The last cartographer worked by candlelight, though electricity was still available. It was a choice, and she was aware of it.

Her maps no longer depicted geography. That work had been finished long ago, filed, and largely ignored. What she drew now were the distances between things—not miles but something else, a measure she had invented and never named.

The city outside her window moved as cities do, which is to say without pattern, without the courtesy of meaning. She watched it sometimes, pencil in hand, trying to find the lines.

She never did. She drew them anyway.

People came occasionally to buy her maps. They asked where the maps led. She told them that was the wrong question. Most of them left. The ones who stayed were the ones who understood, or thought they did, which amounted to roughly the same thing.

She folded each map into quarters before handing it over. She had learned that the fold was part of the meaning—that a map opened was a different object than a map closed, and both were different from the territory, which was different still from the memory of the territory, which was the only version any of them were really navigating anyway.

She worked until the candle went out. She lit another. The city kept moving. She kept drawing.
